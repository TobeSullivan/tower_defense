# Go-to-market — direction note (stub)

Captured 2026-06-05. Direction only; the full plan is its own session.

## Beachhead: Steam / PC-first
The SC2 Random TD audience is on PC (SC2 Discords, TD subreddits), and the marketing machine
that works is Steam-native. With PC-first locked, GTM aligns with both audience and channel.

## Chris Zukowski principles (howtomarketagame.com) that apply
- **Genre defines the audience.** TD is a durable "working" genre (not a fad) — good.
- **Put up the Steam page ASAP** — the instant we're willing to show anything. It's the
  wishlist vessel; a game can go viral anytime and without a page you lose the wishlists.
  **Blocked on the game's NAME** (still TBD).
- **Wishlists are the currency.** ~7,000 → "Popular Upcoming" visibility; the **launch email
  blast** to wishlisters is the single most valuable, unbuyable marketing event — and it only
  converts to revenue because we're a **paid** game ($10–15 PC band).
- **Reality:** ~3% of Steam games reach 1,000 reviews (the "success" benchmark); under 50
  reviews = Steam sends you no traffic. The Steam page must read as "that type of game" in
  ~10 seconds (capsule, tags, trailer).
- **Demo + Next Fest** is the festival entry ticket; most wishlists come from people who never
  play the demo (capsule/screenshots/trailer drive it).

## Next big deliverable: a VERTICAL SLICE
One mode, one map, taken to near-final quality (art, sound, **juice**). It does triple duty:
forces the polish question, sets the quality bar for the rest of production, and **is** the
Steam-page trailer + Next Fest demo. Vertical slice → Steam page → wishlists → launch blast.
This is also the natural home for the queued juice/game-feel pass.
